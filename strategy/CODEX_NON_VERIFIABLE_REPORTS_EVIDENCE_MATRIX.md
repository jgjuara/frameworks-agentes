# Evidence Matrix: Context and Prompting for Non-Verifiable Report Tasks

**Abstract:** This evidence matrix summarizes trusted sources used to evaluate how a research team should prompt Codex for open-ended report generation when the source universe is not exhaustively specified. It prioritizes peer-reviewed or scholarly work, official documentation, standards/government guidance, and primary enterprise guidance.

## Inclusion Criteria

Included sources meet at least one criterion:

- peer-reviewed, conference, preprint, or systematic scholarly work directly relevant to prompting, long-context use, factuality, retrieval, or citations;
- official OpenAI Codex or API documentation;
- official documentation from major enterprise AI providers;
- government or standards guidance on generative AI risk management.

Excluded sources:

- social media commentary;
- generic prompt blogs without primary evidence;
- vendor marketing pages without operational guidance;
- secondary explainers when a primary source was available.

## Strength of Evidence

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Long context alone is insufficient. | High | Supported by long-context research and enterprise prompting guidance. |
| Source grounding and provenance reduce risk. | High | Supported by RAG research, Microsoft guidance, and factuality evaluation literature. |
| Citation hallucination is a material risk in academic-style reports. | High | Supported by empirical citation-fabrication studies. |
| Structured prompts improve reliability. | Medium-high | Strong consensus across documentation and surveys, but effects vary by model/task. |
| Claim-level verification is necessary for high-value reports. | Medium-high | Directly supported by long-form factuality evaluation; operational cost varies. |
| Exact optimal token budget can be stated universally. | Low | Evidence supports principles, not a universal token count. |

## Evidence Gaps

- There is limited public evidence specifically measuring Codex performance on non-code research-report tasks.
- Most prompt-engineering evidence is model-, task-, and time-dependent.
- Enterprise guidance is practical but not always peer-reviewed.
- Long-context behavior changes with model generation, so teams should maintain their own evals.

## Research Implication

The evidence supports an internal evaluation program. A research team should test prompt templates on its own common report types, because "best" context length and prompt structure will depend on domain, risk tolerance, available source databases, and reviewer standards.
