# Codex Context and Prompting for Non-Verifiable Research Reports

**Abstract:** This report evaluates how much context and prompting a research team should give Codex when asking it to create a text report on a subject without an exhaustive, taxative source index. The evidence supports a bounded-context operating model: give Codex a compact task brief, explicit source-quality rules, a staged workflow for source discovery and synthesis, and a final claim-level verification pass. More context is useful only when it is relevant, structured, provenance-bearing, and reviewable.

## Executive Summary

For non-verifiable report-writing tasks, the research team should not treat context size as the main control variable. The stronger control is **context quality**: source provenance, task boundaries, output schema, uncertainty rules, and explicit verification requirements.

Recommended default:

- Initial prompt: **600-1,200 words** for the task brief.
- Persistent repository guidance: **short `AGENTS.md` plus task-specific markdown files**, not one large prompt.
- Source context: **a curated source inventory**, not pasted bulk documents unless the task requires close reading.
- Report-generation workflow: **source discovery -> source screening -> outline -> claim ledger -> draft -> citation audit -> final report**.
- Final review: **claim-level evidence checking**, because polished reports can be wrong even when they cite real sources.

This differs from a simple "give Codex everything" approach. Long-context research shows that models can fail to use information reliably when the relevant facts are buried in the middle of large contexts. Enterprise prompting guidance also stresses specificity, order, grounding data, citations, and giving the model an explicit "not found" path.

## Problem Definition

The target task is a non-verifiable or weakly verifiable report request such as:

> "Create a report about subject X. Use reliable sources, but I am not giving you an exhaustive source list."

This is risky because the model must decide:

- what counts as the subject boundary;
- what sources are authoritative;
- which claims are important;
- how to resolve contradictions;
- whether missing evidence means "unknown" or "not searched enough";
- how to avoid fabricated or unsupported citations.

The task is not fully non-verifiable in principle: individual sources, quotations, citations, and factual claims can be checked. What is non-verifiable is the completeness of the source universe and the adequacy of synthesis. That makes the right process closer to a literature-review workflow than a free-form writing task.

## Evidence-Based Findings

### 1. Bigger context windows do not eliminate context-engineering risk

Liu et al. show that language models can perform worse when relevant information appears in the middle of long contexts, even for models designed for long-context use. The implication for Codex is direct: dumping many documents into context can create false confidence if the agent is not also told how to prioritize, extract, and cite evidence.

Source: Liu et al., "Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts"  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172

### 2. Long-form factuality needs claim decomposition and search-backed checking

Wei et al. propose SAFE, which breaks long-form answers into individual facts and checks support through search. Their benchmark treats long-form factuality as a set of fact-level support decisions rather than a single global judgment. For a research team, that supports a claim ledger and citation audit after drafting.

Source: Wei et al., "Long-form factuality in large language models"  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.18802

### 3. LLMs can fabricate citations and errors in credible-looking reports

A Scientific Reports study found substantial rates of fabricated and erroneous citations in ChatGPT-generated academic-style papers. The paper reports that in its sample, 55% of GPT-3.5 citations and 18% of GPT-4 citations were fabricated, and many real citations still had substantive errors. This is directly relevant to literature-report tasks.

Source: Walters and Wilder, "Fabrication and errors in the bibliographic citations generated by ChatGPT"  
https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-41032-5

### 4. Retrieval and provenance are central for knowledge-intensive tasks

Lewis et al. introduced retrieval-augmented generation for knowledge-intensive NLP and emphasized the limitations of relying only on parametric model memory, including provenance and updating world knowledge. For research reports, Codex should browse or inspect supplied sources and record source provenance instead of relying on memory.

Source: Lewis et al., "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks"  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401

### 5. Prompting is a structured discipline, not a bag of magic phrases

The Prompt Report surveys prompt engineering and identifies a broad taxonomy of prompting techniques. The relevant conclusion is operational: prompt design should be systematic, explicit, and evaluated, especially when tasks are open-ended and high judgment.

Source: Schulhoff et al., "The Prompt Report: A Systematic Survey of Prompt Engineering Techniques"  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.06608

### 6. Codex-specific guidance favors concise durable instructions and scoped task prompts

OpenAI Codex best practices recommend giving Codex the right task context with four core elements: goal, context, constraints, and done-when criteria. They also recommend using `AGENTS.md` for reusable team guidance and avoiding prompt overload by moving durable rules into `AGENTS.md` or skills.

Sources:  
https://developers.openai.com/codex/learn/best-practices#strong-first-use-context-and-prompts  
https://developers.openai.com/codex/learn/best-practices#make-guidance-reusable-with-agentsmd  
https://developers.openai.com/codex/learn/best-practices#common-mistakes

### 7. Enterprise guidance converges on grounding, citations, structure, and explicit failure modes

Microsoft's Azure OpenAI prompting guidance recommends specificity, attention to order, grounding data for reliable answers, inline citations for claims, and giving the model an explicit "not found" path. It also notes that grounded source material closer to the final answer form reduces model work and error opportunity.

Source: Microsoft Learn, "Prompt engineering techniques"  
https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/foundry/openai/concepts/prompt-engineering

Anthropic's long-context guidance recommends structuring documents with metadata and asking the model to extract relevant quotes before the final task. Its agent-evaluation guidance also stresses that "well-sourced" and "correct" are context-dependent and require a mix of code-based, model-based, and human evaluation.

Sources:  
https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/claude-prompting-best-practices#long-context-prompting  
https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/demystifying-evals-for-ai-agents

Google's Gemini Enterprise prompt guide repeatedly models workflows where the agent locates official guidelines, rubrics, source documents, historical benchmarks, and then drafts using the evidence extracted from prior steps. This supports decomposition for report-writing rather than one-shot generation.

Source: Google Cloud, "Prompt guide for Gemini Enterprise"  
https://cloud.google.com/gemini-enterprise/resources/prompt-guide

### 8. Risk management guidance treats generative AI as a lifecycle system

NIST's Generative AI Profile is a companion to AI RMF 1.0 and frames trustworthy AI use around design, development, use, and evaluation. For Codex report writing, this means the team should govern the workflow, not only the prompt.

Source: NIST AI 600-1, "Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework: Generative Artificial Intelligence Profile"  
https://www.nist.gov/publications/artificial-intelligence-risk-management-framework-generative-artificial-intelligence

## Recommended Framework

Use a three-layer context model.

| Layer | What It Contains | Recommended Size | Why |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Durable team context | `AGENTS.md`, source standards, report quality rules, citation policy | 1-3 pages | Prevents repeated prompting and keeps norms stable |
| Task brief | question, audience, scope, exclusions, source hierarchy, output format, acceptance criteria | 600-1,200 words | Gives Codex the operating target without burying it |
| Evidence context | source inventory, source notes, extracted quotes/facts, contradiction log | variable | Grounds claims and makes review possible |

## Prompt Content That Matters Most

A good report prompt should include:

1. **Decision purpose:** why the report is being produced.
2. **Audience:** who will read it and what they need to decide.
3. **Scope and exclusions:** what belongs outside the report.
4. **Source hierarchy:** peer-reviewed first, official sources second, top enterprise research third, weaker sources only for context.
5. **Search protocol:** where Codex may search and how it should screen sources.
6. **Uncertainty rules:** distinguish evidence, inference, speculation, and unresolved conflict.
7. **Citation rules:** no citation unless the source was opened or supplied; inline citations near supported claims.
8. **Output schema:** abstract, executive summary, methods, evidence table, synthesis, recommendations, limitations, source list.
9. **Verification:** create a claim ledger and check each important claim against a source.
10. **Stop conditions:** ask before using low-trust sources, making legal/medical/financial claims, or treating weak evidence as settled.

## How Much Context to Provide

There is no universal token count. The working rule should be:

> Provide the minimum context that makes the intended output, source policy, and review criteria unambiguous, then let Codex gather or inspect source evidence in a staged way.

Practical defaults:

- **Short task, low risk:** 300-600 words, 3-5 source constraints, one output template.
- **Research memo:** 600-1,200 words, explicit source hierarchy, target length, evidence table required.
- **Literature review:** 1,000-2,000 words plus search protocol and inclusion/exclusion rules.
- **High-stakes report:** staged workflow only; require source inventory approval or at least a separate evidence matrix before drafting.
- **Long supplied documents:** attach or paste only if needed; require document metadata and quote extraction before synthesis.

Avoid:

- one massive prompt with durable team rules, task instructions, sources, and style preferences mixed together;
- relying on "be accurate" or "do not hallucinate" without source and citation mechanics;
- asking for a polished report before source discovery and screening;
- accepting end-of-document bibliographies without checking inline claim support.

## Recommended Codex Workflow

1. **Intake**
   - User gives the task brief.
   - Codex restates scope, assumptions, source hierarchy, and risk class.

2. **Source Discovery**
   - Codex searches trusted sources.
   - It produces a source inventory with reason for inclusion or exclusion.

3. **Evidence Extraction**
   - Codex extracts key facts, quotes, methods, population, dates, and limitations.
   - It records contradictions and weak evidence.

4. **Synthesis Plan**
   - Codex proposes report outline and claim categories.
   - User can redirect before prose is drafted.

5. **Draft**
   - Codex writes with inline citations and separates evidence from interpretation.

6. **Verification**
   - Codex creates a claim ledger: claim, source, support status, confidence, action.
   - Unsupported claims are removed, qualified, or moved to open questions.

7. **Finalization**
   - Codex writes the final markdown report.
   - Final response lists files changed and verification limitations.

## Recommended Default Prompt Template

```markdown
# Research Report Task

## Objective
Create a report that informs [decision/use case].

## Research Question
[Question.]

## Audience
[Who will read it and what they need.]

## Scope
Include:
- [Include.]

Exclude:
- [Exclude.]

## Source Hierarchy
1. Peer-reviewed scholarship and systematic reviews.
2. Official government, standards, or institutional publications.
3. Top enterprise research/engineering publications from primary organizations.
4. Other sources only when used for background and clearly labeled.

## Source Rules
- Search for sources; do not rely on memory for factual claims.
- Do not cite a source unless you opened or were provided the source.
- Prefer primary sources over summaries.
- Record contradictory evidence.
- If evidence is missing, write "not found" or "uncertain"; do not fill gaps.

## Method
First produce a source inventory and evidence matrix.
Then draft the report.
Then produce a claim ledger checking major claims against sources.

## Output
Write markdown with:
- Header and abstract.
- Executive summary.
- Methods.
- Evidence synthesis.
- Recommendations.
- Limitations.
- Source list.

## Done When
- The source inventory is complete for the stated scope.
- Every important claim has nearby citation support.
- Unsupported claims are removed or qualified.
- The limitations section states what was not verified.
```

## Governance Recommendation for a Research Team

Adopt a standard operating procedure:

- Put durable research-integrity rules in `AGENTS.md`.
- Create a `research-report` skill or template for repeated report work.
- Require an evidence matrix before final report drafting for medium/high-risk tasks.
- Require a claim ledger for any report used externally or for decision-making.
- Keep one Codex thread per task to avoid bloated context.
- Maintain a small library of accepted source hierarchies by domain.

## Bottom Line

For non-verifiable report tasks, the correct answer is not "give Codex a larger prompt." The correct answer is:

> Give Codex a compact but explicit task brief, make source discovery and evidence extraction separate deliverables, require inline claim support, and finish with a claim-level verification pass.

That workflow uses Codex's strengths while controlling the known failure modes: long-context underuse, fabricated citations, unsupported synthesis, and confident but unverifiable prose.
